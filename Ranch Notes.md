## Misc Ranch Items

### Chestnuts (Spider Repellant Only)

- Not grown at the ranch
- Grown at the estate
- Not used in cooking or baking
- Used exclusively as spider repellant
- Placed in corners, closets, drawers, and the storm cellar
- Household utility item, not a crop

### Ranch Onions

- Green onions were grown at the ranch.
  - Used for salads, green chile enchiladas, garnish, and Papaw’s lunch ritual.
  - Also used fresh in summer cooking.
  - Grown in garden rows or windowsill pots.

- Yellow and white onions were NOT grown at the ranch.
  - Barbara bought them by the bushel from a man in Hobbs.
  - Used for okra gumbo, meatloaf, stuffed bell peppers, pickles, and everyday cooking.
  - Always purchased, never planted.

### Family Variants

- One ranch family grew only pumpkins and corn.
  - No beans, no squash, no tomatoes.
  - Relied on trade or purchase for everything else.

### Papaw’s Lunch Tradition

- Every lunch included:
  - one green onion
  - one serrano pepper
  - cherry peppers
- Regardless of the main meal.
- Reflects ranch crop priorities and family food culture.

### Ranch Food Taboos

- Pickled okra was absolutely forbidden.
  - Making or serving it was considered a family excommunication offense.
  - Okra was eaten fried, stewed, or in okra gumbo only.

- Pickled eggs were equally forbidden.
  - Eggs were eaten fresh only (scrambled, fried, boiled, deviled, or in baking).
  - No jarred or preserved egg dishes were allowed.

### Ranch Pepper Practices

- Red bell peppers were mesquite smoked and ground into paprika.
  - Family tradition, rarely known outside the ranch.
  - Explains why red bells were grown instead of green.

- Cherry peppers were grown specifically for Papaw.
  - Part of his daily lunch ritual.
  - Eaten fresh as a side dish.

- Green chile (Hatch) was grown and used in enchiladas, casseroles, salsa, and gumbo.
  - Never called “Hatch,” just “green chile.”

- Jalapeños and serranos were everyday peppers.
  - Serranos were Papaw’s second side dish.

- Poblanos were never grown.
  - Not suited to South Plains climate.
  - Not part of ranch cooking.

### Pepper Mislabeling Incidents

- One year, Daddy was sold Anaheim pepper plants instead of standard green chile.
  - They grew fine but did not taste the same.
  - Family verdict: “okay, but not green chile.”

- Red bell pepper seedlings were occasionally mislabeled, with yellow bells slipped into the tray.
  - Yellow bells ripen faster and do not smoke or grind into paprika the same.
  - Caused yearly frustration during planting season.

### Ranch Tomatoes

- Beefsteak tomatoes were grown for slicing and raw eating (burgers, sandwiches, fresh plates).
- Cherry tomatoes were grown for salads, tuna salad, and snacking.
- A third tomato variety was grown specifically for cooking (okra gumbo, canning, stews).
  - Usually a firm, low-water variety such as Rutgers, Celebrity, Better Boy, or Homestead.
  - This tomato held its shape and made the gumbo base.

### Corn (Dual Category Crop)

Corn is listed in both Spring Planting and High-Yield Production Crops due to its dual role in ranch life.

- Our family planted corn each spring for fresh eating and roasting.
- Another ranch family grew large fields of corn:
  - Feed corn (low-quality, used for livestock)
  - Flour corn (dried and ground)
  - Roasting corn (shared with neighbors)
- Corn served both household garden needs and large-scale production purposes.
