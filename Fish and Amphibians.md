### Amphibians

- Tiger Salamander (Ambystoma mavortium)
  - Thick, glossy bodies in black or dark olive with yellow blotches
  - Wide heads, small eyes, and smooth skin
  - Move with slow, deliberate motions
  - When wet, shine like polished stone
  - Found all over northern NM
  - Can survive cold water
  - Often live in burrows near lakes
  - Breed in shallow, sandy edges
  - You’d rarely see them — they’re secretive
  - Hide in damp soil near the lake edge
  - Emerge only during rain or Valley‑active nights
  - Their presence is felt more than seen
  - Size: 6–11 inches
- Western Toad (Anaxyrus boreas)
  - Warty, earth‑toned skin in browns and greens
  - Pale belly
  - Horizontal pupils
  - Backs often carry a faint pale stripe
  - Look like small lumps of forest floor until they move
  - Lives at high elevation
  - Breeds in cold lakes
  - Tadpoles tolerate low temps
  - Adults stay mostly on land
  - Could appear near the meadow edge or forest boundary
  - Calls are soft and infrequent
  - Size: 2–5 inches
- Northern Leopard Frog (Lithobates pipiens)
  - Bright green or brown
  - Bold dark spots ringed in pale gold
  - Long legs built for quick jumps
  - Smooth skin that glistens near water
  - Eyes are large and expressive.
  - Spotted, quick, and alert
  - Found in NM mountain wetlands
  - Needs slightly warmer shallows
  - Could exist if the Valley “allows” it
  - Would stay near the porous outlet or sun‑warmed edges
  - Presence indicates a healthy shoreline
  - Size: 2–4.5 inches

### Cructaceans

- Freshwater Shrimp (Gammarus / Scuds)
  - Tiny, translucent crustaceans that resemble miniature curled shrimp
  - Dart in quick, jerky motions
  - Often pale green or tan, blending with algae
  - Size: 0.3–0.6"
- Crayfish (Northern Crayfish)
  - Rust‑brown or olive bodies
  - Strong pincers
  - Hide under rocks and outcrop shelves
  - Slow walkers, fast swimmers when startled
  - Size: 2–5"
- Freshwater Snails (Physa / Pond Snails)
  - Small, spiral‑shelled grazers
  - Shells range from tan to dark brown
  - Cling to rocks near the spring and porous inlet
  - Size: 0.2–1"

## ⭐ The lake stays balanced because

- Fish migrate out through the underground river  
  Prevents overcrowding and keeps populations moving.
- Trout occasionally eat smaller fish  
  Opportunistic predation on fry, eggs, and juveniles.
- Cold water slows reproduction  
  Natural population control for all species.
- Limited spawning areas prevent overcrowding  
  Sandy shallows + cold depth = controlled breeding.
- The Valley itself maintains ecological balance  
  Selective access prevents invasive species and overuse.
- Animal predation keeps populations in check
- Kaya takes fish occasionally and disturbs fry/shoreline.
- Bears (when allowed) take trout seasonally.
- Wolves disturb shallows and larvae when they pass through.
- Birds take insects, larvae, and occasionally small fish.
- Human predation (Lea) is minimal and intentional
  - Lea fishes only what she needs.
  - Uses traps and refrigeration responsibly.
  - Never overharvests.
  - Her presence adds natural disturbance cycles without harming the ecosystem.

### Plant Species

#### 1. 🌱 Submerged Aquatic Plants (Underwater)

- Pondweed (Potamogeton spp.)
  - Long, ribbon‑like leaves
  - Grows in shallow to mid‑depth zones
  - Provides shelter for fry
  - Major food source for snails and scuds
  - Exists in both
    - In rivers, it grows in slower pools and eddies
    - In lakes, it grows in mid‑depth zones
- Water Milfoil (Myriophyllum sibiricum)
  - Feathery, soft underwater foliage
  - Grows near the porous underground river inlet
  - Excellent habitat for insect larvae
  - Exists in both
    - In rivers, it anchors in gravel pockets
    - In lakes, it grows near the porous inlet
- Stonewort (Chara spp.)
  - Looks like bright green underwater moss
  - Actually a type of algae
  - Thrives in cold, mineral‑rich spring water
  - Cleans the lake by absorbing nutrients
  - Exists in both
    - Loves mineral‑rich water
    - In rivers, it forms mats in calm stretches
    - In lakes, it thrives near the spring
- Elodea (Elodea canadensis)
  - Dense green clusters
  - Common in cold lakes
  - Tadpoles hide in it
  - Snails graze on its surfaces
  - Exists in both
    - In rivers, it grows in sheltered bends
    - In lakes, it forms dense clusters

#### 2. 🌾 Emergent Plants (Grow at the Water’s Edge)

- These are essential for insects, amphibians, and shoreline stability.
- Sedges (Carex spp.)
  - Grass‑like plants with triangular stems
  - Grow in sandy shallows
  - Provide egg‑laying sites for insects
  - Frogs hide among them
  - Exists in both
    - River sedges grow thicker and taller
    - Lake sedges stay low and clustered
- Rushes (Juncus spp.)
  - Round stems, dark green
  - Grow near the porous outlet
  - Stabilize shoreline
  - Shelter for crayfish and small fish
  - Exists in both
    - River rushes stabilize banks
    - Lake rushes stabilize shallows
- Cattails (Typha latifolia) (limited presence)
  - Only in the warmest shallows
  - Provide nesting material for birds
  - Tadpoles feed on decaying cattail matter
  - Need still or slow water
  - Rivers wash them out
  - Only appear in very slow river backwaters
  - Lake has them in warm pockets
- Water Smartweed (Persicaria amphibia)
  - Floating leaves and pink flower spikes
  - Attracts pollinators
  - Provides shade for fry
  - Exists in both
    - River smartweed grows in slow backwaters
    - Lake smartweed grows near warm pockets

#### 3. 🍃 Floating Plants (Surface Layer)

- Water Shield (Brasenia schreberi)
  - Small oval leaves
  - Jelly‑coated stems
  - Provides shade for fry
  - Insects lay eggs on the underside
  - Needs calm surface
  - Lake only
- Duckweed (Lemna minor) (minimal)
  - Tiny floating green dots
  - Food for tadpoles and insects
  - Only appears in warm pockets near the meadow edge
  - Needs still water
  - Cannot survive in fast current
  - Lake only

#### 4. 🌿 Algae & Microflora (Base of the Food Web)

- These are the true foundation of your ecosystem.
- Exists in both as universal food sources
- Green Algae
  - Grows on rocks near the spring
  - Primary food for snails and scuds
- Diatoms
  - Golden‑brown microalgae
  - Thrive in cold, clear water
  - Essential food for fry and crustaceans
  - Rivers have MORE diatoms due to current
- Periphyton
  - Soft, greenish film on rocks
  - Tadpoles graze on it
  - Indicates a healthy, oxygen‑rich lake

#### 5. 🌲 Shoreline Plants (Indirect Food Sources)

- These feed insects, which feed fish.
- Willow (Salix exigua)
  - Thin, flexible branches
  - Grows near riverbanks
  - Drops insects into the water
  - Provides shade for trout
  - Rivers have willow roots in the water
  - Roots create hiding places for fry and crayfish
- Rocky Mountain Maple (Acer glabrum)
  - You already have this as the Landing Branch
  - Drops seeds and insects into the lake
  - Supports caterpillars and pollinators
- Wild Mint (Mentha arvensis)
  - Grows near damp soil
  - Attracts bees
  - Insects fall into the water → fish food
- Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)
  - White flower clusters
  - Supports pollinators
  - Seeds and insects drift into the river
- Blue Flag Iris (Iris missouriensis)
  - Grows in wet meadow edges
  - Attracts dragonflies
  - Dragonflies feed on mosquitoes → balance
  - Prefers wet meadows and riverbanks
  - Rare around lake edges unless a meadow touches it

#### 6. 🌊 RIVER‑ONLY (Rare or absent in lakes)

- These plants need moving water, gravel beds, or riffles.
- River Moss (Fontinalis antipyretica)
  - Dark green, feathery moss
  - Clings to rocks in fast current

- Water Buttercup (Ranunculus aquatilis)

## Migration/Spawning/Hibernation Dates

### Winter (Dec–Feb)

- Lake freezes except near spring and porous inlet
- Fish move deep; feeding slows
- Amphibians enter dormancy
  - **Tiger Salamanders**
    - Burrow deep into soft soil
    - Under roots, logs, or shoreline banks
    - Sometimes 12–24 inches underground
    - Enter a state called brumation (cold‑weather dormancy)
    - Metabolism drops to almost nothing
    - Absorb moisture from the soil to avoid drying out
    - Do not stay in the water during winter — they hide in the earth like little dragons waiting for spring
  - **Western Toads**
    - Dig into loose soil, leaf litter, or under logs
    - Sometimes use abandoned rodent burrows
    - Stay just below the frost line
    - Enter brumation
    - Remain completely hidden until spring thaw  — they’re basically little earth‑colored lumps tucked into the ground.
  - **Northern Leopard Frogs**
    - Overwinter in the lake or river, but:
    - Settle on the bottom
    - Stay in oxygen‑rich zones
    - Absorb oxygen through their skin
    - Remain motionless for months
    - Avoid deep, stagnant pockets
    - Rely on your spring + porous inlet for oxygen
    - They do not bury themselves in mud — that would suffocate them.
    - They simply sit on the lakebed like spotted stones, breathing through their skin.
- Crustaceans remain active under ice

### Spring (Mar–May) – Major Spawning Season

- Rainbow Trout: Mar–Apr
- Cutthroat Trout: Apr–May
- Brook Trout: May
- Perch: Apr–May
- Smallmouth Bass: May–Jun
- Tiger Salamander: Mar–Apr
- Western Toad: Apr–May
- Leopard Frog: May

### Summer (Jun–Aug)

- Catfish spawn: Jun–Jul
- All fish feed heavily
- Amphibians active
- Crustaceans peak

### Fall (Sep–Nov) – Major Spawning Season

- Brown Trout: Sep–Oct
- Brook Trout (second run): Oct
- Kokanee Salmon: Oct–Nov
- Juvenile trout migrate out through underground river
- Amphibians prepare for winter
