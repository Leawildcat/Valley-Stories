### D. Lake & Campfire/Campsite

- **Features:**
  - Mostly round
  - Extended ends around the rocky outcrop at one end and where it flows into pourous area for underground river
  - Geological Origin:
    - Formed from a collapsed limestone cavern whose roof fell in centuries ago
    - Edges reinforced by volcanic basalt intrusions from ancient regional activity
    - Creates the “eye‑shape” illusion from above
  - Size:
    - Approx. 110–140 ft diameter at widest point
    - Narrowest point near the willow and porous outlet: 18–22 ft across
  - Depth:
    - Shallows: 0–3 ft
    - Mid‑zone: 3–8 ft
    - Deep drop: ~30 ft
  - Porous Outlet:
    - Natural limestone break forming a large vertical fissure
    - Water drops into an underground waterfall/shelf
    - Underground river flows beneath forest and cliff
    - Re‑emerges at about 300 yards from BLM campsite to form tributary to main river where land naturally slopes below underground river
    - Joins the main river just past the BLM campsite
  - Lake has mirror surface
  - Contains fish and crustacrans
  - Amphibians present
  - Temperature at deeper depth cool enough for natural refrigeration
  - Rock containment firepit and Mini-Oven
    - Circular stone‑laden firepit
    - One side contains a flat‑bottomed oblong cavity carved into sandstone and limestone
    - Dolomite stones used around the cavity for heat retention
    - Sparse basalt inclusions present from ancient volcanic activity
    - Functions as a natural mini‑oven for baking bread, bannock, frybread, and herb loaves
    - Limestone insulates heat and moisture
    - Dolomite withstands repeated heating
    - Sandstone provides a smooth carveable interior
    - Basalt pockets add additional heat stability where naturally present
    - More modern metal hanging 'spit' for roasting, hanging pots from
    - Two extended flat rocks that act as stove burner handing over coals
  - Flat rock “table height” part of the outcrop on edge
  - Saddle bag, basket, ropes station
  - Maple tree used for Guardian Landing Branch drying rack
  - Stumps used as tables
  - Logs used as seating
  - Teepee (future addition for smoking meats)

- **Canon:**  
  - The lake amplifies sound and light in certain emotional states.
  - The fire reflects perfectly in the lake, creating the illusion of two fires.
  - Pourous outlet creates tributary river in BLM campsite
  - Cougar can jump narrowest distance cleanly
  - Rock containment firepit and Mini-Oven carved generations ago by Lea’s ancestors
  - One log has hidden cache with latch that Lea uses to store personal items.
  - Hydrology = Spring in Cavern>Lake>Pourous Oulet>Undergorund River> above ground BLM tributary>River
  - Kevin recognizes the firepit as the kind of stone circle he’s only ever seen in old westerns

### E. Ridgeline (Cougar Ridge)

- **Features:**
  - Sits to the right‑front quadrant of the Valley when entering from the aspens
  - Not as far right as the willow
  - Not directly behind the lake
  - Slightly southeast of the campfire
  - Forms the lower eyelid of the Valley’s “eye” shape when viewed from above (Chapter 7)
  - Stratified rock formation composed of:
    - Sandstone layers (white, tan/yellow and red)
    - Dolomite bands
    - Limestone pockets
    - Shale and slate seams
    - Quartzite shelves (hard gray stone)
    - Basalt intrusions from the Chama–Taos–Raton volcanic arc
  - Ridge height:
    - Approximately 90–120 ft above the Valley floor
    - Tall enough for Jack (raven) to glide down effortlessly
    - Tall enough that Lea (cougar) requires four jumps to reach the forest floor
  - Ridge shape:
    - Long, slightly curved formation
    - Curves inward toward the lake like a protective arm
    - Creates natural amphitheater acoustics
  - Ledges:
    - Multiple sunning shelves for cougars
    - One broad quartzite shelf used repeatedly
    - Narrow basalt ledges that fracture cleanly
  - Surface texture:
    - Wind‑carved pockets
    - Shallow caves and overhangs
    - Smooth quartzite plates
    - Rough sandstone edges
  - Vegetation:
    - Sparse pines and junipers clinging to cracks
    - Scrub brush and grasses
    - Occasional wildflowers in shale pockets
  - Orientation:
    - Faces the lake directly
    - Overlooks campfire, meadow, and forest
    - Provides full view of the Valley’s “eye” layout but only when sun hits just right at sunset or sunrise
  - Access:
    - From the forest floor (Valley side):
      - Only one steep access path
      - Frequently washes out and makes footing treacherous
      - Too steep for horses
      - Natural switchback path formed by erosion
    - From top of ridge to edge overlooking the Valley and forest:
      - Two paths
      - Easy access from Frank's house
      - Short distance from Frank's house
    - Forest Floor at bottom Valley side
      - Clearing for grazing animal
      - 2 paths back to Valley proper:
        - One for smaller animals
        - Larger and wider one for horses and humans
  - Large oval boulder on top of ridge marks trail head to forest floor

- **Canon:**  
  - Access:
    - From the forest floor (Valley side):
      - Rarely used by humans
      - Used mainly by Kaya to visit Selby and Marino's Home
    - From top of ridge to edge overlooking the Valley and forest:
      - Favorite lookout and thinking spot of Frank
      - Aids in optical illusion of the Valley being hidden and forest compressed
      - Trail head used as shifting area for Jack and Lea if no humans present
  - Jack and Lea prefer crossing it animal forms
